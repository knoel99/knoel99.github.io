Conclusion, limites & perspectives

Where the roadmap meets the research frontier

This closing section maps the territory the authors have not yet conquered — and that mapping is itself informative. Each announced direction corresponds to a known bottleneck in the current architecture: MoE and sparse attention buy efficiency in compute, but embedding tables remain dense memory hogs; long-context works but latency makes deployment painful; single-turn reasoning is strong but agentic multi-round orchestration remains fragile; the model is text-only; and data quality ceilings intelligence regardless of architecture. Read this way, the conclusion is less a wish list and more a diagnosis of the five pressure points that currently limit the system.

The emphasis on sparsity along new dimensions — specifically sparse embeddings — signals that the team views sparsity not as a one-time architectural trick but as a design principle with multiple levers. The mention of Cheng et al. (2026), a future-dated reference, suggests this is work already in progress rather than speculative. The commitment to low-latency systems work acknowledges a hard truth: efficient inference at long context lengths is as much a systems problem as a model problem, and architectural innovation alone won't close the gap.

"We recognize the importance and practical value of long-horizon, multi-round agentic tasks, and will continue to iterate and explore in this direction."

This is the most strategically significant sentence. Agentic capability — sustained, tool-using, multi-step autonomy — is where the field is converging, and the authors are explicit that their current model, while strong on benchmarks, has not yet cracked it.

Why these five directions specifically?

Each direction maps to a distinct bottleneck class: (1) memory — dense embeddings don't scale; (2) latency — long-context is computationally viable but too slow for real-time use; (3) robustness — single-turn benchmarks don't capture agentic failure modes; (4) modality — text-only limits real-world grounding; (5) data — architecture improvements yield diminishing returns without better training signals. The authors are covering the full stack: from hardware-aware systems through model architecture to data quality, which suggests a team that has hit walls at every level and is now pushing on all of them simultaneously.
