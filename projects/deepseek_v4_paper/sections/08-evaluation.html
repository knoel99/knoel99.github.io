Reading Angle: The Reasoning-Effort Dial as the Real Architecture

The benchmarks tell one story — DeepSeek-V4-Pro-Max is competitive with closed models — but the deeper story is about what variable actually drives the performance curves. This section reveals that the model's three reasoning-effort modes (Non-Think → High → Max) function less like presets and more like a compute-allocation architecture layered on top of the neural architecture. Each mode reconfigures context windows (8K → 128K → 384K), RL length penalties, and tool-access budgets simultaneously. The jumps aren't incremental: HLE goes from 7.7% to 37.7% on Pro, and Codeforces rating leaps from nothing to 3206. This is the paper's quiet argument — that inference-time scaling is now a first-class design axis, not an afterthought.

The real-world evaluations (Chinese writing, white-collar tasks, internal R&D coding) serve a different purpose than the benchmarks. They are the authors' answer to the growing distrust of standardized benchmarks — the acknowledgment that leaderboard numbers and user satisfaction diverge, especially in non-English, non-code domains. The human-evaluated white-collar tasks expose the model's actual failure modes: formatting aesthetics (76.68 vs 72.68 — both mediocre), instruction-following on hard constraints, and over-condensation. These are precisely the weaknesses that no benchmark in Table 6 would surface.

The formal math result (120/120 on Putnam-2025) is strategically placed: it demonstrates that the hybrid informal-formal pipeline — where natural-language drafts guide a Lean prover — is the path to mathematical reliability, not raw scale alone. This mirrors the broader thesis: hybrid orchestration beats monolithic inference.

Why the Codeforces Elo calculation is unusually careful

The rating methodology — generating 32 candidates, sampling 10 without replacement, randomizing order, scoring via human penalty schemes, then averaging expected ratings across 14 contests — is designed to neutralize the "volume advantage" that LLMs have over humans. A human gets one submission sequence; a model could submit thousands. By sampling a fixed subset and randomizing order, the authors approximate what rating the model would earn if it were a human participant making a single contest attempt. This is why the resulting 3206 Elo is meaningful: it's not inflated by brute-force submission volume.

The informal→formal math pipeline as a design pattern

The Putnam-2025 result (120/120) relies on a two-stage pipeline: first generate and self-verify informal natural-language proofs, then feed survivors as guidance to a Lean formal agent. This is architecturally significant — it treats informal reasoning as a heuristic search pruner and formal verification as the correctness guarantee. The same pattern (cheap exploration → expensive validation) appears in the agentic search design (RAG for simple queries, iterative agentic search for complex ones) and in the reasoning-effort dial itself (Non-Think for easy, Max for hard). It's the paper's recurring motif: allocate compute proportionally to problem difficulty, and never trust without verification.
