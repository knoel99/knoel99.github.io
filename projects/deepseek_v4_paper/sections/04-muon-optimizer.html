Alternative Reading Angle: Muon Optimizer

Commentary

This section sits at the heart of DeepSeek-V4's training efficiency gains. The core question: can you replace AdamW — the default workhorse — with something that converges faster without blowing up, at the scale of hundreds of billions of parameters?

Think of Muon as a gradient orthopedist. AdamW treats each parameter independently, adjusting step sizes per-coordinate. Muon instead looks at each weight matrix as a whole and asks: "what direction should this matrix move, ignoring magnitude?" The Newton-Schulz iterations strip away the singular values, leaving only the orthogonal component UVᵀ — the pure rotational direction. The two-stage hybrid scheme is like downshifting: aggressive coefficients slam singular values toward 1 for 8 steps, then gentler coefficients lock them in place for the final 2, avoiding overshoot.

The Nesterov trick (evaluating momentum at a lookahead point) and RMS rescaling are not novel here — they are compatibility shims. The rescaling factor √max(n,m) · γ ensures the update magnitude matches what AdamW would produce, so the team can reuse their carefully tuned hyperparameters rather than re-searching the entire learning rate schedule. Keeping AdamW for embeddings, heads, and normalization layers is a pragmatic hedge: those modules have different spectral structure and are cheap relative to the attention/MLP bulk, so the risk-reward of switching isn't worth it.

The RMSNorm-on-QK trick is elegant: rather than patching exploding logits after the fact with QK-Clip, the architecture itself prevents the pathology, which is cleaner and avoids gradient interference from clipping operations.

Why not just use the aggressive coefficients for all 10 steps?

The aggressive (a, b, c) = (3.4445, −4.7750, 2.0315) coefficients have high gain, which is great for rapid convergence but can cause singular values to oscillate around 1 rather than settling exactly at 1. The gentler (2, −1.5, 0.5) coefficients have lower gain and are contractive near the fixed point σ = 1, ensuring the final output is a clean orthogonal matrix. This two-stage approach mirrors the classical numerical analysis pattern of using a fast-but-rough solver followed by a slow-but-exact refinement — similar to using a high-learning-rate warmup followed by decay.
