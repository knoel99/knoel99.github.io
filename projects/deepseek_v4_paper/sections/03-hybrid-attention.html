Alternative Reading Angle: Hybrid Attention (CSA & HCA)

This section is the engineering heart of DeepSeek-V4's million-token capability. The fundamental problem: standard attention scales quadratically, so at extreme context lengths the attention layer alone becomes computationally prohibitive. The authors respond with a dual-compression architecture that treats distant context not as something to attend to faithfully, but as something to summarize first, then search.

Think of it like a library. CSA is the card catalog: it compresses books into index entries (m tokens → 1), then the Lightning Indexer acts as a search engine that retrieves only the k most relevant entries. HCA is the encyclopedia summary: it crushes huge swaths of text into single entries (m' >> m tokens → 1) and keeps them all, accepting heavy information loss for guaranteed coverage. The sliding window is your reading desk — the last few pages you're actively working with, kept at full resolution. The genius is that these two strategies cover complementary failure modes: CSA can retrieve specific distant facts but might miss something if the indexer fails; HCA always provides something from every region but at low fidelity.

The authors chose overlapping compression in CSA (each compressed entry draws from 2m source tokens with half-overlap) precisely to avoid boundary artifacts — the "crack" between adjacent compressed blocks. HCA forgoes this luxury because its role is coarse coverage, not precise retrieval. The shared-key-value MQA trick (compressed entries serve as both key and value) is a pragmatic sacrifice: it halves the representation capacity per entry, but the compression has already destroyed most fine-grained information anyway, so separate K and V would be redundant overhead.

CSA vs. HCA at a Glance

Property CSA HCA
Compression ratio m tokens → 1 m' tokens → 1 (m' ≫ m)
Overlap in compression Yes (2m source, half-overlap) No
Sparse selection Yes — Lightning Indexer picks top-k No — all compressed entries retained
Role Precise retrieval of relevant distant context Dense coarse coverage of all context
KV cache reduction (1M ctx) ~1/m × baseline ~1/m' × baseline (most aggressive)
Shared KV (key = value) Yes Yes
Sliding window branch Yes Yes
Why overlapping compression in CSA but not HCA?

CSA's overlapping window means each source token contributes to two adjacent compressed entries. This eliminates the "boundary problem" where a relevant fact split across two blocks might be poorly represented in both. Since CSA's job is selective retrieval, boundary artifacts would directly cause retrieval failures. HCA skips overlap because (a) its heavier compression already loses far more information than any boundary effect, and (b) it keeps all entries, so there's no retrieval step that boundary artifacts would disrupt — every region contributes something to the attention output regardless.

The attention sink trick explained

Standard softmax forces attention weights to sum to 1. The attention sink adds a learnable term Exp(z'_h) to the denominator, allowing the total attention weight on actual tokens to be less than 1. This is crucial for compressed attention: if none of the compressed entries are truly relevant to a query, forcing it to attend heavily to irrelevant entries injects noise. The sink gives the model an "escape valve" — it can choose to mostly ignore the compressed context and rely on the sliding window or residual pathways instead.
